Blog

Bias in AI means that sometimes computer programs make unfair choices. This happens because the computers learn from trained data, and if that data contains unfairness, it replicates it. So, if a computer learns mostly from one type of data, it might not understand or assist with other types of data as well. This can lead to problems like unfair job hiring or loan decisions. People are trying to address this issue in several ways, such as improving diversity in AI design teams so that different viewpoints are included from the start to enhance the accuracy of the data.

. Additionally, preprocessing of the data is done to measure and correct for bias before training the AI. Various techniques, like reweighting data or generating new sets of vetted data, are employed. Rigorous testing of AI systems is conducted before and after deployment using methods like adversarial testing and independent audits. This allows for checking bias against protected groups. Making AI systems more transparent and explainable facilitates the analysis of their internal processes. Approaches like model cards provide details on an AI's construction and limitations, while maintaining human oversight and judgment in AI-assisted decisions ensures fairness.

Determining when autonomous systems are permissible requires ethical frameworks. Educating the public on how algorithms use their data, given their increasing prevalence in daily life, is crucial. This algorithmic literacy helps people understand potential biases. Overall, rectifying unfair bias requires ongoing work across an AI system's entire lifecycle, considering both technical solutions and social factors like transparency and diversity.
